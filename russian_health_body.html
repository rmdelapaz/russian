🇷🇺 Lesson 10: Health & Body

🎯 What You'll Learn

Being able to describe symptoms and communicate health needs is vital when traveling. This lesson covers body parts, common symptoms, pharmacy vocabulary, the dative case for expressing how you feel, and emergency phrases.

Estimated Time: 45–60 minutes

💡 Note: Рука and Нога

Рука means both "arm" and "hand" — Russian doesn't distinguish between them as separate words. Context clarifies which is meant. Similarly, нога means both "leg" and "foot."

✅ Grammar Note: Dative Case for Feelings

Russian uses the dative case to express how someone feels:

Мне холодно (I'm cold, lit: "to me it is cold")

Мне жарко (I'm hot)

Мне плохо (I feel bad)

Мне хорошо (I feel good)

The pattern: dative pronoun + adverb. No verb "to be" needed!

💡 Cultural Insight: Russia has a two-tier healthcare system. Поликлиника (polyclinic) is the outpatient clinic for regular visits — every neighborhood has one. Больница (hospital) is for inpatient care. Russian pharmacies (аптека) sell many medications without prescription that would require one in the West, and pharmacists often give medical advice directly. Home remedies are also extremely popular — hot tea with мёд (honey) and малина (raspberry jam) is the national cold cure!

🗣️ Practice Dialogue

— Здравствуйте, доктор. Мне плохо.

(Zdrastvuytye, doktar. Mnye plokha.) — Hello, doctor. I feel unwell.

— Что у вас болит?

(Shto u vas balit?) — What hurts?

— У меня болит горло и голова. И температура.

(U minya balit gorla i galava. I timpiratura.) — My throat and head hurt. And I have a fever.

— Когда это началось?

(Kagda eta nachalos'?) — When did it start?

— Вчера вечером.

(Fchira vyechiram.) — Yesterday evening.

— Похоже на простуду. Я выпишу лекарство.

(Pakhozhye na prostudu. Ya vypishu likarstva.) — Looks like a cold. I'll prescribe medicine.
